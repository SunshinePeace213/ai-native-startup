## 1. Business & goals

_Understanding what Bluebird Bakery does and what this website must achieve._

- **What does Bluebird Bakery do, and who do you serve today — walk-in neighborhood customers, event and wedding orders, wholesale accounts, or a mix?**
  _Why we ask: it anchors every later decision to your actual business._
  >
- **What is the single most important thing this website must accomplish in its first six months — for example, more custom cake inquiries, more walk-in traffic, or fewer phone calls asking for hours and directions?**
  _Why we ask: it forces one primary goal to prioritize scope against._
  >
- **How will you know the website worked — more orders, more foot traffic, more catering inquiries, or something else?**
  _Why we ask: it turns "a website" into a measurable business outcome._
  >
- **Is this a brand-new website, a redesign of an existing site, or a replacement for something that isn't working for you today (an outdated site, or relying on a Facebook or Instagram page)?**
  _Why we ask: it sets baseline expectations and migration risk._
  >

## 2. Audience & users

_Who this site is for and what each visitor needs to get done._

- **Who are the two or three main types of people who'll visit this site — for example, neighborhood regulars checking hours, someone planning a birthday or wedding and looking for a custom cake, or a business ordering pastries for an office — and what is each one trying to do?**
  _Why we ask: these personas drive the site's structure, copy, and features._
  >
- **Will most of your customers be checking the site on their phone (on the way to the shop, deciding where to stop) or on a desktop computer (planning a bigger order in advance)?**
  _Why we ask: it sets the mobile-versus-desktop priority._
  >
- **What is the one action you most want each visitor to take — call the bakery, get directions and hours, submit a custom order or catering request, or something else?**
  _Why we ask: it defines the primary path we design toward._
  >
- **Is your customer base mostly local to your neighborhood, or do you also want the site to reach visitors, tourists, or customers who'd prefer information in a language other than English?**
  _Why we ask: it flags localization and language needs early._
  >

## 3. Brand & voice

_How the site should look and sound._

- **Do you have brand guidelines, a logo, and a color palette already, or do those need to be created for this project?**
  _Why we ask: it separates applying your brand from defining it from scratch._
  >
- **In three words, how should the site feel to a first-time visitor — warm and homey, upscale and artisanal, playful and fun, or something else?**
  _Why we ask: it gives our designers a concrete tone target._
  >
- **Are there other bakery, café, or restaurant websites whose look you admire, or ones you'd want yours to avoid resembling?**
  _Why we ask: it calibrates aesthetic direction quickly._
  >
- **Is there approved copy, a tagline, or specific wording we must use verbatim — for example, a family story, "since [year]," or a recipe's origin?**
  _Why we ask: it locks non-negotiable wording before we start drafting._
  >

## 4. Content & assets

_What content already exists, and who's producing what's missing._

- **What content already exists — menu descriptions, ingredient or allergen lists, product photos, your logo — and who owns each piece?**
  _Why we ask: it distinguishes what we reuse from what we need to create._
  >
- **Who will write and approve the words on the site — you, or us?**
  _Why we ask: it assigns copy responsibility and affects the schedule._
  >
- **Do you have professional photography of your baked goods and shop, or should we plan around stock imagery or placeholders until you do?**
  _Why we ask: photos of the product are central to a bakery site — this shapes both design and cost._
  >
- **How often does your content change after launch — daily specials, seasonal items, holiday menus — and who will make those updates?**
  _Why we ask: it drives what the site needs to let you edit yourself versus what stays static._
  >

## 5. Structure & pages

_The shape of the site and how people move through it._

- **What are the must-have pages for launch — for example Home, Menu, About/Our Story, Location & Hours, Order/Catering, Contact?**
  _Why we ask: it defines the minimum footprint we build._
  >
- **How do you picture someone moving from the homepage to the action you want — browsing the menu first, or going straight to a contact or order form?**
  _Why we ask: it surfaces the primary flow we should design for._
  >
- **Are there sections you're unsure about that we should check with you before building — a full online store, a blog, a loyalty page?**
  _Why we ask: it flags soft scope worth confirming before we commit to it._
  >
- **Do you have an existing website, or a Facebook/Instagram page acting as one, whose structure we should keep or deliberately break from?**
  _Why we ask: it manages migration and sets customer-habit expectations._
  >

## 6. Features & functionality

_What the site needs to do beyond a menu and photos._

- **Beyond static content, what must the site do — online ordering for pickup or delivery, a custom cake or catering inquiry form, gift card purchases, a newsletter signup, a loyalty program?**
  _Why we ask: it separates a brochure site from something closer to an application, which changes cost and timeline significantly._
  >
- **For each of those, is it required for launch, or something we can plan for a later phase?**
  _Why we ask: it keeps the first release focused and on budget._
  >
- **Have you seen features on other bakery or food-business sites that you consider essential for yours?**
  _Why we ask: it grounds expectations in concrete references rather than abstract wishes._
  >
- **What should happen when someone submits a custom order or catering inquiry — should it email you directly, should the customer get an automatic confirmation, does it need to reach a specific staff member?**
  _Why we ask: it defines the success and failure states of your most important form._
  >

## 7. Integrations & data

_The tools this connects to and where information ends up._

- **What other tools does the site need to connect to — a point-of-sale system (Square, Toast, Clover), an online ordering platform, email marketing, an Instagram feed, Google Business Profile / reviews?**
  _Why we ask: integrations carry the biggest hidden technical cost of the project._
  >
- **Where does information a customer enters need to end up — your email inbox, a shared order spreadsheet, your POS system, a newsletter list?**
  _Why we ask: it defines destinations, ownership, and any compliance surface._
  >
- **Do you already have accounts set up for those tools, or do some need to be created before we can connect them?**
  _Why we ask: it flags access and credential blockers early._
  >
- **Is there existing data we need to bring over — a customer email list, an existing menu spreadsheet, content currently living only on Facebook or Instagram?**
  _Why we ask: migration is a common source of schedule risk._
  >

## 8. Technical & hosting constraints

_The technical ground rules._

- **Do you already have a domain name and hosting, or do those need to be arranged as part of this project?**
  _Why we ask: it determines launch logistics and where the site will live._
  >
- **Is there a platform or technology you must use or must avoid — for example, a preference for something you can edit yourself, like Squarespace or Wix, versus a custom build?**
  _Why we ask: hard constraints bound the whole solution._
  >
- **Who will maintain the site technically after launch — you or a family member updating the menu and hours yourselves, or should we plan for an ongoing support arrangement?**
  _Why we ask: it sets the maintainability and handoff bar._
  >
- **Are there accessibility or performance standards you need to meet — for example, a fast-loading mobile site, or ADA/WCAG accessibility, which is a common legal consideration for public-facing food businesses?**
  _Why we ask: non-functional requirements like these shape the whole build, and accessibility gaps are a common source of legal exposure for small businesses._
  >

## 9. Budget & timeline

_The time and money we're working within._

- **Is there a hard launch date or event this must be ready for — a grand opening, an anniversary, or the start of a busy season like holiday baking?**
  _Why we ask: a fixed date forces scope tradeoffs._
  >
- **What budget range are we working within?**
  _Why we ask: budget bounds the feasible scope and quality._
  >
- **If we had to choose, is hitting the date or delivering the full feature set more important to you?**
  _Why we ask: it pre-agrees the tradeoff before schedule pressure hits._
  >
- **Would you be open to a phased launch — a simpler site now, with features like online ordering added later?**
  _Why we ask: it opens up a smaller, faster first release as an option._
  >

## 10. Success metrics

_How we'll know it worked, in numbers._

- **How will you measure whether this website was worth it — what number do you want to see move?**
  _Why we ask: it makes success testable rather than a feeling._
  >
- **What is that number today, and where would you like it to be?**
  _Why we ask: a baseline and a target are what make a metric measurable._
  >
- **How will we track it — your POS system's reports, website analytics, counting form submissions, or something else?**
  _Why we ask: a metric only helps if it's observable after launch._
  >
- **By when do you expect to see that change — a month after launch, a full season, a year?**
  _Why we ask: it sets the window we'll use to judge success._
  >

## 11. References & competitors

_Where you sit in your neighborhood and your market._

- **Who are the other bakeries or cafés you consider competitors, and what do their websites do well or badly?**
  _Why we ask: it positions your site in its actual market._
  >
- **Show us two or three sites — bakery, café, or otherwise — you'd love yours to stand beside.**
  _Why we ask: concrete references beat abstract adjectives._
  >
- **Is there a look or a specific site we should make sure NOT to copy or resemble?**
  _Why we ask: negative references sharpen the differentiation._
  >
- **In one sentence, what makes Bluebird Bakery different — your recipes, sourcing, family history, a specialty item?**
  _Why we ask: your differentiator becomes the hero message on the site._
  >

## 12. Legal & compliance

_The legal and regulatory details, easy to overlook until launch._

- **Do you need a privacy policy, terms of use, cookie consent, or accessibility statement on the site?**
  _Why we ask: legal pages are easy to forget and costly to add after the fact._
  >
- **Will the site collect any sensitive information — for example, payment details for online orders, or allergy/dietary details on a custom order form?**
  _Why we ask: any regulated or sensitive data changes how we design data handling and where it can be stored._
  >
- **Do you need to display allergen information (nuts, gluten, dairy, eggs, etc.), a health department permit, or any certifications (organic, kosher, and similar) on the site?**
  _Why we ask: food-related disclosures are often a practical necessity, and sometimes a legal one, for a bakery's public-facing site._
  >
- **Who signs off on legal content — the privacy policy, terms, any required disclosures — before we launch?**
  _Why we ask: it assigns approval ownership so this doesn't stall the launch._
  >
