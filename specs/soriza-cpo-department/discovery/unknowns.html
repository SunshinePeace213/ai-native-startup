The vocabulary of product & design work

You said you have no idea what a real company looks like. Here is the mental model, the words professionals use, and the quality bar they judge by — enough to prompt the next pass with real vocabulary.

Mental model — four corrections

  1. You're describing a studio, not a product company

    A CPO exists where a company owns its own product and roadmap. Soriza does client work — in the real world that is an agency / design studio, run by a Creative Director, a Strategist, and a Producer. The client owns "what"; the studio owns how it's discovered, defined, designed, and delivered. Keep the CPO name if you like it — but staff the department with studio-shaped roles, or the titles will pull the workflow toward roadmaps and backlogs you don't have.

  2. All design work runs the double diamond

    Discover (learn the client's world) → Define (agree the brief) → Design (wireframe → mockup → prototype) → Deliver (handoff). Your workflow step 1 is Discover + Define; your step 3 is Design; the future CTO department is Deliver. Naming the phases lets each one have its own gate.

  3. Fidelity is a ladder you climb with the client

    Brief → sitemap/IA → wireframe → mockup → prototype. Each rung locks one class of decision so the next rung is cheap. Jumping from brief straight to a polished prototype means the client reacts to structure, layout, and color all at once — and every revision is expensive.

  4. "Did the client give us enough?" is a solved problem

    It's called client intake plus a Definition of Ready: a discovery questionnaire that fills the brief, and a checklist that must pass before design starts. Your instinct to want "our own standards" is exactly right — you just don't need to invent the concept, only Soriza's version of it.

Job titles — reality check

Product OwnerA Scrum-framework role, not an org title. In a small studio it duplicates the Product Manager — pick one.

Missing entirelyThe client-facing side every agency has: Account Manager / Producer — owns client communication and scheduling. Your "cookbook for talking to clients" is this role's playbook, not the PM's.

The quality bar professionals judge by

  • A designer who never met the client could design from the brief alone.
  • IA is settled before wireframes; wireframes are approved before any visual design.
  • Every section brief names that section's single job and the one action it should cause.
  • Prototypes use real copy and real client assets — never lorem ipsum.
  • One design system feeds every client project; assets are tokens and components, not a folder of downloads.
  • Every client round ends with a written decision log: what was approved, what changed, what's open.
